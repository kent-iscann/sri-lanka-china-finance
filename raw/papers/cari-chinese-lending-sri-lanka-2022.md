# Evolution of Chinese Lending to Sri Lanka Since the mid-2000s
**Source:** China-Africa Research Initiative (CARI), Johns Hopkins SAIS, Briefing Paper No. 8, November 2022
**Authors:** Umesh Moramudali and Thilina Panduwawala

## Key Findings

- China is Sri Lanka's largest bilateral creditor.
- The event of default clauses in the four Hambantota Port China Eximbank loans did NOT feature any asset seizure clauses, and port assets were NOT included as collateral.
- In the year the port was leased to China Merchant Ports, port loans accounted for only 2.4% of Sri Lankan government's total foreign debt repayments.
- Chinese lending at end of 2021 came to 19.6% of public external debt, higher than the often-quoted 10-15% figures.
- During 2008-2021, the effective interest rate on overall Chinese lending averaged 3.2%, higher than Japanese (0.9%), World Bank (1.6%), and ADB loans, but significantly lower than Eurobonds which averaged 6.9%.
- Sri Lanka's debt crisis was multi-causal: 47% of external debt was owed to international capital markets, 22% to multilateral banks, and about 10-20% to China.

## Loan Data Highlights

The paper provides detailed tables of Chinese loans to Sri Lanka from the mid-2000s through 2021, including:
- Hambantota Port Phase I (2007): $307m from China Exim Bank
- Norochcholai Coal Power Plant (2006): $1.35 billion from China Exim Bank
- Mattala Rajapaksa International Airport (2010): $191m from China Exim Bank
- Colombo-Katunayake Expressway (2009): $248m from China Exim Bank
- Hambantota Port Phase II SOT (2012): $390m from China Exim Bank
- Colombo Port City (2012): $1.4 billion from China Development Bank
- Lotus Tower (2012): $104m from China Exim Bank
- Hambantota Industrial Zone (2014): $200m from China Exim Bank
