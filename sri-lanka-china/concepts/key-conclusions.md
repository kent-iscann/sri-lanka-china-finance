# Key Conclusions on China-Sri Lanka Financial Relations

Synthesized conclusions drawn from multiple sources on the financial relationship between China and Sri Lanka. Where sources disagree, both positions are noted.

## 1. The Debt Crisis Was Multi-Causal

Sri Lanka's 2022 sovereign default cannot be attributed to any single creditor or policy. The crisis resulted from the convergence of multiple factors:

- **Structural economic weaknesses**: An import-oriented economy dependent on tourism and remittances, with limited manufacturing capacity, was inherently vulnerable to external shocks.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- **Western commercial borrowing**: 47% of external debt was owed to international capital markets. International Sovereign Bonds (ISBs) carried an average interest rate of 6.9% -- far higher than Chinese loans at 3.2%.^[raw/papers/cari-chinese-lending-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- **Multilateral institution debt**: 22% of external debt was owed to multilateral banks like the ADB and World Bank.^[raw/papers/cari-chinese-lending-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- **Chinese lending**: Approximately 10-20% of public external debt was owed to China by 2021.^[raw/papers/cari-chinese-lending-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- **COVID-19 pandemic**: Devastated tourism and remittances, two critical sources of foreign exchange.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- **Domestic policy failures**: Decades of fiscal mismanagement, tax cuts, and politically motivated "white elephant" projects by successive governments.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]

**Conclusion**: Blaming Sri Lanka's crisis on China alone is analytically flawed. Chinese lending was a contributing factor but not the primary driver.

## 2. The Debt-Trap Diplacy Debate Remains Genuinely Contested

There is no scholarly consensus on whether China practiced "debt-trap diplomacy" in Sri Lanka:

**Evidence supporting the debt-trap narrative:**
- The 99-year lease of Hambantota Port to China Merchants Port is widely cited as a case of asset seizure through debt entrapment.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- Chinese loans carried higher interest rates (3.3%) and shorter maturities (18 years) compared to Japanese (0.7%, 34 years) and Indian (24 years) loans.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- China was initially reluctant to restructure Sri Lanka's debt during the crisis, unlike India which was more generous.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]

**Evidence against the debt-trap narrative:**
- The Hambantota Port loan agreements contained NO asset seizure clauses and the port was NOT included as collateral.^[raw/papers/cari-chinese-lending-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- Sri Lanka actively persuaded China to finance Hambantota after India and Japan declined. The 2016 lease was also sought by Sri Lanka after India and Japan again declined to take it on.^[raw/papers/hkust-debt-trap-myth-sri-lanka-2019.md]
- The lease was NOT a debt-for-equity swap: China Merchants Port paid $1.12 billion -- more than the port's valuation -- for a 70% share. Sri Lanka retained 3% plus rights to buy more.^[raw/papers/hkust-debt-trap-myth-sri-lanka-2019.md]
- At the time of the lease (2016), only 9% of government external debt was owed to China. The SLPA could cover Hambantota loan payments from Colombo Port profits.^[raw/papers/hkust-debt-trap-myth-sri-lanka-2019.md]
- 99-year port leases are not unusual: Australian states have leased multiple major ports for 99 years to entities of varied nationality.^[raw/papers/hkust-debt-trap-myth-sri-lanka-2019.md]
- No national asset anywhere outside China has been forfeited to Chinese lenders. China has restructured loans for scores of debtor states.^[raw/papers/hkust-debt-trap-myth-sri-lanka-2019.md]

**Conclusion**: The debt-trap narrative is an oversimplification. The reality involves a complex interplay of Sri Lankan agency, Chinese commercial interests, and structural economic factors. The debate has significant political dimensions, with the narrative being promoted by US elites and allies as part of counter-mobilization against the BRI.^[raw/papers/hkust-debt-trap-myth-sri-lanka-2019.md]

## 3. Sri Lankan Agency and Domestic Politics Were Central

The relationship was driven as much by Sri Lankan domestic politics as by Chinese strategic interests:

- The Rajapaksa family (Mahinda and Gotabaya) actively courted Chinese financing for politically motivated projects in their home district of Hambantota.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- Projects like Mattala Airport and Hambantota Port were widely seen as "vanity projects" intended to boost the Rajapaksas' image among supporters rather than serve national economic needs.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- Sri Lanka's civil war (1983-2009) created the initial conditions for the relationship: Western nations were less willing to provide support, and China filled the gap with both military and financial assistance.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- Even after the Rajapaksas lost power in 2015, the Sirisena-Wickremesinghe government continued borrowing from China, suggesting the relationship was not solely a product of one family's preferences.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]

## 4. China's Image in Sri Lanka Has Been Damaged, but Influence Will Persist

- China's credibility as a lender took a severe beating during the 2022 crisis. Anti-China sentiment emerged in Sri Lanka for the first time during the Aragalaya protests.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- India was seen as more generous during the crisis, and China's initial reluctance to restructure debt was noted by other BRI countries.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- Despite this, Chinese influence in Sri Lanka is likely to continue because: (1) only China has the deep pockets to fund large-scale infrastructure without political conditions, (2) the Rajapaksa family may return to power, and (3) IMF conditions may redirect public anger away from China.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- As security analyst Nilanthi Samaranayake noted: "Regardless of who is in the domestic political leadership, any Sri Lankan leader will need to work with China as a critical development partner."^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]

## 5. The Crisis Has Implications for the Belt and Road Initiative

- Other BRI countries (Bangladesh, Nepal, Myanmar, Ethiopia) are watching Sri Lanka's experience closely and adjusting their borrowing strategies accordingly.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- Bangladesh has been careful to maintain healthy forex reserves; Nepal prefers grants over commercial loans.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- The crisis will make BRI countries more cautious borrowers, but given their need for infrastructure financing and the lack of alternative sources with comparable scale, dependence on China will continue.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- Sri Lanka's experience has sullied China's image as a lender not just in Sri Lanka but across the developing world.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]

## 6. The Debt Restructuring Process Is Largely Complete

- As of late 2024, Sri Lanka completed major debt restructuring agreements with both Chinese policy banks (China Exim Bank and China Development Bank), international bondholders (98% participation), and the Paris Club.^[raw/papers/cari-chinese-lending-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- The IMF's $2.9 billion Extended Fund Facility, approved in March 2023, was contingent on progress with debt restructuring.^[raw/papers/cari-chinese-lending-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- As of 2025, Sri Lanka is in recovery, though GDP has not yet returned to 2018 levels.

## 7. The India-China Rivalry in Sri Lanka Continues

- Sri Lanka has become a key arena for India-China strategic competition in the Indian Ocean.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- India has pushed back against Chinese influence through agreements on Trincomalee Oil Tank Farm and the West Container Terminal at Colombo Port.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- Unlike during Mahinda Rajapaksa's second term (2010-15) when India lost ground, the post-2019 period has seen India recover some strategic footing in Sri Lanka.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- Sri Lanka's post-crisis leadership has sought a more balanced relationship with both powers compared to the strongly pro-China stance of the Rajapaksa era.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
