# The Debt-Trap Diplomacy Debate

The question of whether China practices "debt-trap diplomacy" -- deliberately lending to developing countries with the expectation of default in order to seize strategic assets -- is one of the most contested topics in contemporary international political economy. Sri Lanka, and specifically the Hambantota Port case, is the most frequently cited example.

## The Debt-Trap Narrative

Proponents of the debt-trap narrative argue that:

- China extends loans to developing countries for infrastructure projects that are commercially unviable, knowing the borrower will struggle to repay.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- When the borrower defaults, China seizes the asset, gaining strategic control (the "debt-for-equity" model).^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- The 99-year lease of Hambantota Port to China Merchants Port Holdings is the paradigmatic example.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- Chinese loans carry less favorable terms (higher interest rates, shorter maturities) compared to other bilateral creditors like Japan.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- The narrative has been promoted by senior US officials including Vice President Mike Pence and Secretary of State Mike Pompeo.^[raw/papers/hkust-debt-trap-myth-sri-lanka-2019.md]

## The Counter-Narrative

Scholars and analysts who challenge the debt-trap narrative argue that:

- **No evidence of deliberate entrapment**: The Hambantota Port loan agreements contained NO asset seizure clauses and the port was NOT included as collateral.^[raw/papers/cari-chinese-lending-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- **Sri Lankan agency**: Sri Lanka actively sought Chinese financing for Hambantota after India and Japan declined. The 2016 lease was also initiated by Sri Lanka after India and Japan again declined.^[raw/papers/hkust-debt-trap-myth-sri-lanka-2019.md]
- **Not a debt-for-equity swap**: China Merchants Port paid $1.12 billion for a 70% stake -- more than the port's valuation. Sri Lanka retained 30% plus rights to buy more. The loan debt was not erased.^[raw/papers/hkust-debt-trap-myth-sri-lanka-2019.md]
- **Modest debt exposure**: At the time of the lease (2016), only 9% of government external debt was owed to China. The Sri Lanka Port Authority could cover Hambantota loan payments from Colombo Port profits (~$67m/year).^[raw/papers/hkust-debt-trap-myth-sri-lanka-2019.md]
- **Concessional terms**: About two-thirds of Chinese loans to Sri Lanka are concessional, averaging 3.2% interest with 20-year repayment periods -- far cheaper than Eurobonds at 6.9%.^[raw/papers/hkust-debt-trap-myth-sri-lanka-2019.md]^[raw/papers/cari-chinese-lending-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- **No assets forfeited**: No national asset anywhere outside China has been forfeited to Chinese lenders. China has restructured loans for scores of debtor states.^[raw/papers/hkust-debt-trap-myth-sri-lanka-2019.md]
- **99-year leases are common**: Australian states have leased multiple major ports for 99 years to entities of varied nationality (local, UAE, Canadian, Indian, Chinese). India and Canada also permit 99-year port leases.^[raw/papers/hkust-debt-trap-myth-sri-lanka-2019.md]
- **Political instrument**: The CDT narrative is part of a US-led counter-mobilization against the BRI and reflects elements of Yellow Perilism and racist ideology.^[raw/papers/hkust-debt-trap-myth-sri-lanka-2019.md]

## The Political Dimension

The debt-trap debate is not purely academic. It has significant geopolitical implications:

- The narrative is promoted by the US, India, Japan, and Australia as part of broader strategic competition with China.^[raw/papers/hkust-debt-trap-myth-sri-lanka-2019.md]
- Opposition parties in BRI countries invoke the debt-trap narrative to discredit ruling parties that seek Chinese loans.^[raw/papers/hkust-debt-trap-myth-sri-lanka-2019.md]
- The narrative shapes how developing countries approach Chinese borrowing, potentially depriving them of needed infrastructure financing.^[raw/papers/hkust-debt-trap-myth-sri-lanka-2019.md]
- Specialists in China's overseas relations increasingly reject the debt-trap framing, though it remains influential in media and policy circles.^[raw/papers/hkust-debt-trap-myth-sri-lanka-2019.md]

## Scholarly Middle Ground

Some analysts occupy a middle position:

- China's lending practices are commercially motivated rather than strategically predatory, but the lack of transparency and environmental/social safeguards creates real problems.^[raw/papers/cari-chinese-lending-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- The Hambantota case shows that Chinese lending can enable poor domestic governance and white-elephant projects, even if China did not deliberately create the trap.^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
- The crisis was multi-causal: structural economic weaknesses, Western commercial borrowing at high interest rates, the pandemic, and domestic policy failures all played larger roles than Chinese lending.^[raw/papers/cari-chinese-lending-sri-lanka-2022.md]^[raw/articles/jamestown-bri-blowback-sri-lanka-2022.md]
