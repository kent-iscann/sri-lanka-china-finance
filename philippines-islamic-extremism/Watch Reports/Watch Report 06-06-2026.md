## Justification

### Political
The September 2026 BARMM parliamentary election is the most consequential political event in Mindanao since the 2019 plebiscite that created the autonomous region. The MILF, which leads the transitional Bangsamoro Transition Authority (BTA), faces mounting internal rivalries over candidate slates, resource allocation, and the balance of power between traditional commanders and political operators. The Marcos administration's appointment of a new peace adviser (Mel Sarmiento) months before the election signals Manila's intent to shape the outcome — raising MILF suspicions of interference. International Crisis Group reporting confirms intra-MILF fissures are widening. The peace adviser's target of a 2028 exit agreement is ambitious and implies the transitional period will need extension beyond the original timeline.

The Marcos-Duterte domestic rupture adds a layer of instability: Duterte's political base in Mindanao means the feud has regional security implications, potentially complicating military-community relations in counter-terrorism operations.

### Economic
BARMM remains one of the poorest regions in the Philippines, with limited fiscal autonomy and heavy dependence on the national government's internal revenue allotment. The transition period's economic underperformance undermines the peace dividend narrative — a key factor in countering extremist recruitment. Disputes over natural resource revenue sharing (particularly minerals and fisheries in the Bangsamoro waters) between Manila and BARMM remain unresolved ahead of the elections, creating friction.

China's economic leverage over the Philippines (as the country's largest trade partner) constrains Manila's freedom of action but does not directly impact Bangsamoro economics. Indirectly, however, South China Sea-related military spending diverts national resources that could support Mindanao development.

### Military
The May 2026 MILF-Army joint front against ISIS-affiliated groups in Lanao del Norte is a historic development — the first operational cooperation between the former insurgent group and the Philippine military against a common extremist enemy. This cooperation signals the MILF's evolution into a legitimate security actor.

However, Abu Sayyaf remnants and ISIS-affiliated cells (particularly the Maute Group splinter factions in Lanao del Sur and Maguindanao) continue low-level operations. The Anti-Terrorism Act of 2020 gives the government expanded powers but has contributed to a tense human rights discourse that complicates military-community relations in Muslim Mindanao.

The Philippine military is currently stretched between South China Sea patrols (in response to China Coast Guard activity) and internal security operations in Mindanao. Election season (September 2026) historically brings a spike in rido (clan feuds) violence in Mindanao, which extremist spoiler groups can exploit.

### Technologically
Extremist recruitment in Mindanao continues to move through encrypted messaging platforms and social media, targeting impoverished youth in conflict-affected areas. The Philippine military's cyber capabilities remain limited relative to this threat. The government's action to place the national budget on blockchain (per FULcrum reporting) is institutionally innovative but has no direct impact on counter-terrorism.

China's digital infrastructure investments in Southeast Asia (under BRI) give Beijing potential surveillance leverage, though this has not been documented in the Philippine context. The broader concern is that Chinese tech partnerships with ASEAN states normalize data-sharing frameworks that could be repurposed.
