# The Middle Corridor

The Middle Corridor (also called the Trans-Caspian International Transport Route) is a multimodal transport route connecting China to Europe via Central Asia, the Caspian Sea, the South Caucasus (Georgia, Azerbaijan), and Turkey — bypassing both Russia and Iran. It has become a central element of Georgia's geopolitical value proposition.

## Route
China → Kazakhstan → Caspian Sea → Azerbaijan → Georgia → Turkey → Europe

## Strategic significance for Georgia
- **Economic:** Transit fees, logistics sector growth, FDI in port/rail infrastructure
- **Geopolitical:** Gives Georgia leverage with both West (alternative to Russian/Iranian routes) and China (land bridge to Europe)
- **US interest:** The Trump administration has emphasized the Middle Corridor as a supply chain diversification priority, partly explaining the US reset with Georgia

## Challenges
- **Capacity:** Still far below the Northern Corridor (through Russia) in volume
- **Bottlenecks:** Caspian Sea crossing, Georgian rail capacity, Turkish border delays
- **Competition:** Armenia-Azerbaijan rapprochement could create alternative routes that bypass Georgia
- **Iran factor:** Disruption of Strait of Hormuz (2026) has increased the Middle Corridor's strategic value

## Key infrastructure
- Baku–Tbilisi–Kars railway (reopened June 2026)
- Batumi and Poti ports
- East-West highway (EU-funded)
- Anaklia Deep Sea Port (planned, Chinese-financed — controversial)
