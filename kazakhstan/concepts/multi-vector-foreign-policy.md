# Multi-Vector Foreign Policy

Kazakhstan's defining foreign policy doctrine since independence. The concept is to maintain productive relationships with all major powers — Russia, China, the US, the EU, and regional actors — without firmly aligning with any single camp.

## Principles
- **Balance:** Avoid over-dependence on any single partner. Russia for security and trade routes, China for investment and BRI, the West for technology and capital.
- **Pragmatism:** Foreign policy serves economic development, not ideology. Kazakhstan will engage with any partner that offers tangible benefits.
- **Sovereignty:** Maintain formal independence in decision-making, even when economic realities create de facto dependence.

## Challenges (2025–2026)
- Russia's sanctions-era pressure is making the balancing act harder. Moscow wants Kazakhstan to serve as a sanctions-bypass corridor; the West wants Kazakhstan to comply with sanctions.
- The margin for error is narrowing. Secondary sanctions risk is real and growing.
- Kazakhstan's authoritarian governance model limits Western engagement depth, pushing Astana closer to Moscow and Beijing by default.

## Iran-Israel War (2025–2026)
- Kazakhstan maintained formal neutrality on the Iran-Israel war, calling for de-escalation and diplomacy.
- In practice, Astana's posture leaned toward Gulf states, the US, and Israel — especially after Iranian retaliatory actions.
- Kazakhstan suspended several joint projects with Iran in April 2026 and emphasized the Middle Corridor.
- Oil price spikes from the conflict benefit Kazakhstan's budget in the short term.
- Kazakhstan participates in the Abraham Accords framework, signaling support for regional normalization without severing ties with Iran.
- Bilateral trade with Israel: ~$236M (2024); with Iran: ~$430M (2025).

## Military modernization
- Tokayev issued a strict two-year deadline for comprehensive military modernization.
- Defense deal with Turkey: ANKA UAV production facility in Kazakhstan.
- Diversifying military imports away from Russia: Chinese CHPL exports to Kazakhstan rose from $610M (2022) to $931M (2025).
- Tokayev downplayed suggestions of a Turkic military alliance while deepening defense partnerships.

## Key question
Whether multi-vector foreign policy can survive the intensifying great power competition, or whether Kazakhstan will be forced to choose sides.
